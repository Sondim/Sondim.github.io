Three Patterns to Explore
The Desire to Remain Unseen: You might endlessly polish your game, delay its release, or post it while trying to blend in and remain invisible. This behavior acts as a defense mechanism to slow down your ambition so you don't have to risk feeling a negative emotion. Ultimately, this fear of being seen is rooted in a belief that there is something inherently wrong with you, and that if people look closely, they will find out you are broken.  

The Preemptive Wall: You assume the audience or forum members will be hostile, critical, or attack your work. However, by being scared of what they will say and assuming the worst, you have actually already judged them, disconnected from them, and turned them into an enemy before they even speak.  

The Identity Merge ("I-ing and My-ing"): We have a strong conditioning to attach our sense of self to our experiences—a habit described as "I-ing" and "my-ing". Because of this, when inevitable pain arises or a project struggles, we take it personally and assume that we are the source of the wrong. We build up ideas of who we are—such as being a competent or successful developer—and then feel we must defend those identities. When you merge your worth with your game, you fear that if the game fails, you will have to feel horrible about yourself.

EXERCISES
1. The "Somatic Negative Review" Warm-upStruggle: Fearing critique and emotional avoidance.  The Experiment: Write the most brutal, unfair, 1-star Steam review of your game that you can imagine. Make it highly personal.  The Action: Read it out loud to yourself.What to Notice: Instead of arguing with the review in your head, close your eyes and find where the "sting" lands in your body. Make a physical sound (a groan, a sigh, or a hum) that matches that feeling. Welcome the physical sensation of "being a failure" for 3 minutes without trying to think your way out of it.

step 2. Humanizing the "Enemy"Struggle: Shifting the perception of an online forum from an "enemy" to "human beings" to drop hyper-vigilance.  The Action:Locate the Hostility: Open the subreddit where you want to post. Scroll until you find a post or comment that feels slightly harsh, cold, or highly critical.  Check Your Armor: Look at the screen and notice your body. Are your shoulders shrugged? Is your jaw clenched? Is your heart closed or hardened? Just say "Ouch" to the tightness.  Uncover Their Struggle: Click on the profile of the person who wrote that comment. Read a few of their other posts or comments in completely unrelated subreddits (e.g., asking for relationship advice, complaining about a job, sharing a hobby).  Open Your Heart: Imagine what this person's physical reality looks like right now—maybe they are stressed, lonely, tired, or just trying to feel a sense of control. Let your jaw relax and open your heart to their human struggle, just like you would with a friend. 

step 3 The 10-Minute "Shitpost" PromotionStruggle: Procrastination, perfectionism, and self-talk blocks.  The Experiment: Bypass the agonizing "marketing prep" spiral with a hard time-limit.  The Action: Set a timer for 10 minutes. Within those 10 minutes, you must capture a screenshot or GIF of your game, write a raw caption, and post it to social media or a discord channel. No proofreading, no aesthetic adjustments. Once the timer dings, you must close the browser tab.What to Notice: Pay close attention to the frantic "it’s not ready/it looks bad" self-talk that hits as the timer winds down. Say "ouch" out loud to every critical thought, post it anyway, and walk away.
