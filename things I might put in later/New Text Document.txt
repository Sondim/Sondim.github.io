But behind that armor, video games became the only place where I could actually find that missing sense of safety. You know what I’ve always wondered? Why do we build things that are designed to trap us?

When I was two, I almost died from a snakebite. Then I got bullied in school and my nervous system took a look at the world and decided: Nope, this place is entirely not safe. So, I built an invisible wall around my life. I wanted connection, love, and ambition, but I was so terrified of being hurt or judged that my strategy was to hide.

And that's exactly how I trapped myself. In trying to avoid the pain of a dangerous world, I ended up creating a completely isolated one. I spent my entire 20s alone, stuck in a job I hated, terrified to spend the money I earned, and totally shut off from my own body. I was just a jittery flesh suit wrapped in a really thick piece of defensive armor, completely dependent on the safety of my room and the comfort of video games to survive.

Videogames are silly when you think about it, but man somehow, when I wasn’t looking, they sneaked in and took a seat right next to all of my biggest feelings.
I've created some of my most sincere memories and made some of my best friends. I still meet those lovable idiots to this day. 
 
That contrast sparked a twenty-year quest where I went into healthcare, treating everything from stiff backs to deep, psychosomatic war trauma. At the same time I worked in the game industry, as a sound designer and composer and I noticed something fascinating. 

The exact same armor I used to wear in my 20s is the exact armor killing game development teams today. We invite the very thing we are running from.

When a dev gets stuck in creative paralysis or endless scope creep, it’s almost never a scheduling problem. It’s that their system is hitting that same invisible wall. They want to ship, but they are so terrified of failure and judgment that their brain screams, “If this fails, we are going to die.” So they hide in endless polishing, rewriting, and delaying. By trying so hard to avoid a potential failure, their defensive strategy guarantees the game never actually comes out. They are driving with the handbrake on to keep safe, trapped by the exact failure they were trying to prevent.

My coaching is just an internal debugging process. We don't "fix" you, because you aren't broken. We just call a truce with the fear, look directly at what you're avoiding with genuine curiosity, and welcome the feeling instead of running from it. When you drop the heavy armor and clear the cache, the internal friction vanishes. Your brain finally has the bandwidth to just create again.

Games are silly, but they're also deeply important. They save people. They saved me.

Don't rob the world of your game. Let's get it built.
